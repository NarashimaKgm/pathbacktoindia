RNOR Status

Last updated: February 2026

RNOR stands for Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident. If you have spent years abroad, you will almost certainly qualify for RNOR status for some period after returning to India. During this window, most of your foreign income is not taxed in India — giving you time to reorganize your finances without being rushed into decisions on day one.

Get a CA involved

RNOR involves income tax, FEMA compliance, and foreign asset reporting — all of which interact in ways that depend on your specific situation. This page gives you a working understanding of the concepts, but you should work with a qualified CA (preferably one experienced with returning NRIs) before making financial decisions.

What RNOR means

Indian tax law puts every individual into one of three buckets each financial year: Non-Resident (NR), Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident (RNOR), or Resident and Ordinarily Resident (ROR). These are determined by how many days you have spent in India in the current and preceding years.

Most people returning from a long stint abroad will start as NR (in the year they return, if they haven't been in India long enough), then move to RNOR for one to three years, and eventually become ROR. The exact number of years depends on your personal travel history — your CA will calculate this for you each year before filing.

What it means for your taxes

As RNOR, your taxable income in India is narrower than it will be once you become ROR:

  • Taxed in India: Income received or accrued in India, and income from a business controlled or profession set up in India.
  • Generally not taxed in India: Foreign income earned and received outside India — such as interest on a US bank account, dividends from US investments, or rental income from a foreign property — as long as it is not linked to a business controlled from India.
  • Reduced reporting obligations: RNOR individuals are generally not subject to the full foreign asset disclosure schedule (Schedule FA) that applies to ROR individuals, though this depends on the ITR form for the relevant year.

RNOR does not mean tax-free

Indian-source income is fully taxable regardless of your residency status. And the interaction between Indian tax, US tax treaties, and foreign tax credits can get complicated. File returns carefully each year and do not assume RNOR exempts you from all obligations.

How to use the RNOR window

The most valuable thing about RNOR is the runway it gives you. Rather than having to restructure everything on arrival, you have time to make considered decisions:

  • Notify your banks: Inform Indian banks of your return and redesignate NRE/NRO accounts as required. Continuing to hold NRE/NRO accounts after becoming resident without informing the bank is a FEMA violation.
  • Consider an RFC account: Returning residents can open a Resident Foreign Currency (RFC) account to park foreign currency balances without converting everything to INR immediately.
  • Hold foreign assets for now: You can generally continue holding overseas brokerage accounts, retirement accounts (like a 401k), and foreign deposits while you plan their long-term structure. Do not liquidate everything hastily.
  • Plan remittances gradually: Bring money to India in stages based on your actual cash flow needs rather than moving it all at once.
  • Prepare for ROR transition: Build a year-by-year plan so that when you do become ROR — and global income and asset disclosure requirements expand — you are not caught off guard.

What to watch out for

A few things that commonly catch returning Indians off guard:

  • US retirement accounts (401k, IRA): India does not automatically recognize the tax-deferred status of these accounts. Interest and growth may technically be taxable in India once you are resident. This is an active area of planning — discuss with your CA early.
  • PFIC rules on US mutual funds: If you hold US mutual funds, be aware they may be classified as Passive Foreign Investment Companies under US tax law, with punitive tax treatment when sold. Get US tax advice before disposing of these.
  • Double taxation: India has a Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA) with the US, which can provide relief. Understanding how it applies to your income types is important.

Practical checklist

  • Engage a CA with NRI/returning resident experience as early as possible.
  • Track your India stay days for each financial year — your CA will need these to determine your status.
  • Confirm your NR/RNOR/ROR status with your CA every year before filing returns.
  • Notify Indian banks of your return and sort out account redesignation.
  • Keep records of all foreign income, taxes paid abroad, and foreign account statements.
  • Review FEMA compliance for foreign assets annually.
  • Start planning your ROR transition 6–9 months before it is likely to happen.

Think of RNOR as a planning runway

The goal is not just to minimize tax in one year, but to arrive at a clean, compliant, and well-structured financial position by the time you become ROR. Use the time well.
