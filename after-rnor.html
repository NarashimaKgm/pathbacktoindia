RNOR Status

Last updated: February 2026

RNOR stands for Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident. This is often a transition status for Indians moving back from abroad. You are tax resident in India, but not yet treated as fully resident for all foreign income and reporting rules.

Note

RNOR is determined separately for each financial year based on your stay pattern and past residency. Many returning Indians get RNOR for a limited period (often 1-3 years), but the exact period depends on your travel history.

What RNOR means

Under Indian tax law, RNOR is available when you become resident but still meet at least one of the additional conditions around your prior non-resident years or days of stay in India.

  • You are first tested for resident/non-resident for the year.
  • If you are resident, a second test decides whether you are RNOR or ordinary resident.
  • This status can materially change what part of your foreign income is taxed in India.

Tax position and scope

As RNOR, your Indian tax base is generally narrower than that of an ordinary resident.

  • Taxed in India: Income received in India, accrued in India, or arising from a business controlled/profession set up in India.
  • Usually not taxed in India: Most foreign income earned and received outside India (for example foreign salary, foreign bank interest, or foreign rental income), unless linked to a business controlled from India.
  • Reporting relief: RNOR individuals are generally outside the full foreign asset disclosure that applies to ordinary residents, subject to the ITR form and applicable year rules.

Warning

RNOR does not mean "no tax." Indian-source income is still fully taxable, and treaty/foreign tax credit positions can still get complex. File returns carefully each year.

Flexibilities during RNOR

RNOR is useful because it gives you time to reorganize finances after relocation instead of making rushed decisions immediately after moving.

  • You can continue holding eligible foreign bank and investment assets acquired while non-resident, subject to FEMA rules.
  • You can plan remittances and transfers to India in a staged way rather than liquidating everything at once.
  • You may use Resident Foreign Currency (RFC) account options offered by banks for returning residents, which can help park foreign currency balances.
  • You can use the RNOR period to align ownership structures, nominee details, and tax documentation before becoming ordinary resident.

What you can do in this window

  1. Keep compliant account status: Inform banks about your return and redesignate NRE/NRO relationships as required.
  2. Retain valid foreign holdings: Continue holding overseas brokerage, retirement, and deposit accounts where permitted, while planning eventual long-term structure.
  3. Receive and use foreign income: Continue receiving eligible foreign income streams and decide timing of remittances to India based on your cash-flow and tax plan.
  4. Structure cash buffers: Keep part of savings in foreign currency (for education/travel/global obligations) and part in INR for local spending.
  5. Prepare for the next phase: Build a year-wise plan for the point when you become ordinary resident and global income/disclosures become broader.

Practical checklist

  • Track your India stay days for each financial year in a spreadsheet.
  • Ask your CA to confirm your status as NR/RNOR/ROR every year before filing.
  • Keep records of foreign income, taxes paid abroad, and account statements.
  • Review FEMA compliance for foreign assets and account types annually.
  • Do a transition review 6-9 months before likely move to ordinary resident status.

Tip

Use RNOR years as a planning runway. The best outcome is not just lower tax in one year, but a clean and compliant long-term structure once you become ordinary resident.
