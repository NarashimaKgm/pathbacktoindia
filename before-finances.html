Finances & Banking

Last updated: February 2026

Getting your finances sorted before you leave is one of the most important — and most stressful — parts of the move. You're essentially unwinding years of financial setup in the US while simultaneously preparing for a new financial life in India. The decisions you make now — what to do with your 401(k), how to move money, which accounts to keep open — will have lasting tax implications in both countries. This page is meant to give you enough context to ask the right questions, not to replace professional advice.

Talk to tax professionals before making financial decisions

Before you close accounts, roll over retirement funds, or transfer large sums — schedule consultation calls with two types of tax professionals:

  • A US-based tax consultant with experience filing and managing non-resident returns, especially for people who have moved to India. They'll help you understand your ongoing US filing obligations, FBAR requirements, and how to handle retirement accounts.
  • An India-based tax consultant with a track record of filing returns and managing international assets for people who have moved back from the US. They'll help you navigate residency rules, DTAA benefits, and how your US assets are taxed in India.

These need to be two separate professionals — a consultant who is excellent at Indian resident tax filings will almost certainly not have the expertise for US non-resident returns, and vice versa. Don't assume your usual tax person can handle both sides just because they're well-known or highly recommended in their area. Look specifically for a track record with cross-border cases involving the US and India.

The information on this page is a starting point to help you have meaningful conversations with those professionals. Every situation is different — your visa status, assets, income sources, and timeline all affect what the right moves are for you. Do your own due diligence and work with qualified professionals before making decisions.

Bank accounts — what to close, what to keep

Your first instinct might be to close everything. Don't. There are good reasons to keep at least one US bank account open. But before you decide which accounts to keep, you need to understand how your residency change affects them.

Non-resident account rules matter

Once you move to India and become a non-resident of the US, any US bank accounts you keep open should technically be converted to non-resident accounts. Non-resident accounts have different tax treatment — interest earned is subject to US withholding tax, and reporting requirements change. Some banks allow existing customers to convert their resident accounts to non-resident status by updating the address to an Indian address after you move. Others strictly do not offer non-resident accounts and will require you to close them before you leave. Factor this into your decision about which bank and which accounts to keep — check your bank's non-resident policy before assuming you can hold on to a particular account or credit card.

  • Keep one checking account open — You'll need it for stray deposits (tax refunds, security deposit returns, final paychecks), recurring subscriptions you haven't cancelled yet, and as a landing pad for any US-based income. Before choosing which bank to keep, confirm they allow non-resident account holders and understand what changes when you update your status.
  • Close everything else — Savings accounts, store cards, and any accounts you don't actively need. Simplify. Each open account is one more thing to monitor from 10,000 miles away.

File Form W-8BEN with every US financial institution

Once you become a non-resident, you need to file Form W-8BEN (Certificate of Foreign Status of Beneficial Owner) with each US bank, brokerage, or financial institution where you hold an account. This form tells the institution that you're a non-resident alien and allows them to apply the reduced withholding tax rate under the US-India DTAA (Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement) instead of the default rate.

Per IRS regulations, non-resident aliens are subject to 30% withholding on the following types of US-sourced income:

  • Dividends
  • Interest (including portfolio interest)
  • Royalties
  • Short-term capital gain distributions from mutual funds
  • Long-term capital gain distributions

With a valid W-8BEN on file, your institution can apply the reduced treaty rate under the US-India DTAA instead of the default 30%. The exact reduced rate depends on the type of income.

  • File it with every institution — Each bank and brokerage needs its own W-8BEN. Filing it with one institution doesn't cover another. If you have a checking account at one bank and a brokerage account elsewhere, both need a W-8BEN.
  • It expires — The W-8BEN is valid for three years from the date of signing. You'll need to re-file it before it expires, or the institution will revert to the default 30% withholding. Set a reminder.

Without W-8BEN, it gets worse — backup withholding on gross proceeds

Beyond the 30% withholding on income, if you don't have a valid W-8BEN on file, your brokerage is also required to apply 24% backup withholding on gross proceeds from transactions like stock sales, mutual fund redemptions, and tender offers. This is withheld on the entire sale amount — not just your profit. So if you sell $100,000 worth of stock, they withhold $24,000 regardless of whether you made a gain or a loss. Getting that money back requires filing a US tax return and claiming a refund, which is a slow and painful process from India. File your W-8BEN before you sell anything.

Credit cards

A US credit history takes years to build and disappears fast. It's worth keeping at least one credit card active, especially if you think you might return to the US someday. But there are a few things to think through.

  • Keep a no-annual-fee card open — Make a small charge on it every few months to keep it active. A dormant card may get closed by the issuer, which hurts your credit score. Set up autopay so you don't miss a payment from India.
  • Check the issuer's non-resident policy — Not all credit card issuers will let you keep the card once your address changes to an international one. Some will close the account or restrict features. Verify this before deciding which card to keep.
  • Redeem or use your rewards before closing cards — Points, miles, and cashback balances often expire or get forfeited when you close an account. Transfer or redeem them before you cancel.
  • Pay off all balances in full — Don't leave the US with credit card debt if you can avoid it. Managing disputes or late fees from India is a headache you don't need.
  • Close store cards and cards with annual fees — If you're not getting enough value from a card to justify the annual fee from India, close it. Store cards (Target, Amazon Store Card, etc.) are rarely worth keeping.

Moving money to India

You'll likely need to transfer a significant amount. Here are your main options:

  • Wire transfer (bank to bank) — The most straightforward option for large amounts. Your US bank sends directly to your Indian bank account (NRE or NRO). Fees range from $15-45 per transfer, and exchange rates from banks are usually not the best.
  • Wise (formerly TransferWise) — Generally offers better exchange rates and lower fees than banks, especially for amounts under $50,000. Transfers typically arrive in 1-2 business days.
  • Remitly, Xe, OFX — Other services worth comparing. Rates and fees vary, so check on the day you're transferring.

Warning

Large transfers may trigger compliance checks on both ends — your US bank may put a hold and your Indian bank may ask for documentation (offer letter, passport, etc.). Don't leave all your money transfers to the last week. Spread them out over time.

NRE vs NRO accounts

Before transferring money, you need to understand where it's going in India:

  • NRE (Non-Resident External) — For money earned outside India. Fully repatriable (you can send it back to the US if needed). Interest earned is tax-free in India. This is where your wire transfers from the US should land.
  • NRO (Non-Resident Ordinary) — For income earned in India (rent, dividends, etc.). Repatriation is limited (up to $1M per financial year with some paperwork). Interest is taxable in India.

Note

Once you become a resident Indian (for tax purposes), your NRE account will need to be converted to a regular resident savings account. The NRO can be converted too. Talk to your Indian bank about the timeline — the rules around when exactly you become "resident" can be nuanced.

401(k) and retirement accounts

This is where many people get stuck. You have a few options, and none of them are perfect:

  • Leave it where it is — You can leave your 401(k) with your former employer's plan (if the balance is above their minimum, usually $5,000). The money continues to grow tax-deferred. This is often the simplest option.
  • Roll it into an IRA — Move it to a Traditional IRA at a brokerage like Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard. Gives you more investment choices and consolidates your retirement savings. Most brokerages will keep your account open even with a foreign address, but some have restrictions — check before you move.
  • Cash it out — You can withdraw the full amount, but you'll pay income tax plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty if you're under 59½. This rarely makes financial sense unless you need the money urgently.

Tip

If you're rolling over to an IRA, do it before you leave the US. Some brokerages make it harder to open new accounts with a foreign address. Fidelity and Schwab are generally more friendly to non-resident account holders.

Tax implications

Moving between countries creates a complicated tax year. Some things to keep in mind:

  • You'll file US taxes for the year you leave — This is a partial-year return covering your US income. You may also need to file a state return depending on your state's rules.
  • US citizens and green card holders must file US taxes forever — Even from India. If you're on H1-B and not a citizen/permanent resident, your US filing obligation generally ends when you leave.
  • FBAR (Foreign Bank Account Report) — If you keep US accounts and the combined balance exceeds $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file an FBAR. This applies even after you move to India if you still have US accounts.
  • India taxes based on residency — Once you're a resident, your worldwide income is taxable in India. The US-India tax treaty helps avoid double taxation, but it's complicated.

Warning

The year you move is the most complex for taxes. Strongly consider hiring a cross-border tax professional who understands both US and Indian tax systems. The cost is worth avoiding mistakes that can be expensive to fix later.

US estate tax for non-residents

This is something most people don't think about until it's too late. Once you become a non-resident of the US, the US estate tax rules change significantly — and not in your favor.

  • Much lower exemption threshold — US residents and citizens get a federal estate tax exemption of over $13 million. Non-resident aliens get only $60,000. That means if you hold more than $60,000 in US-situated assets at the time of death, your estate could owe federal estate tax on the excess — at rates up to 40%.
  • What counts as US-situated assets — This includes cash in US bank accounts, equities held in US brokerage accounts (US-listed stocks, ETFs, mutual funds), US real estate, and tangible personal property located in the US. Essentially, if the asset is "in" the US, it's potentially subject to estate tax.
  • US-listed stocks and ETFs in brokerage accounts — Even if you hold them through a US brokerage like Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard, US-listed equities are considered US-situated property. This is a major consideration if you plan to keep a US brokerage account with a significant portfolio after your move.
  • Bank deposits may have an exception — Deposits in US bank accounts (checking, savings, CDs) that are not effectively connected with a US trade or business are generally exempt from estate tax for non-residents. This is one reason keeping a US bank account is less risky than keeping a large US brokerage account.
  • The US-India tax treaty may help — The DTAA between the US and India has provisions related to estate and inheritance tax, but the specifics are complex and depend on your individual situation. This is exactly the kind of question to bring to your tax professionals.

This affects how much you keep in US accounts

The $60,000 non-resident exemption is surprisingly low. If you're planning to keep a US brokerage account with significant holdings, or maintain large balances in US accounts, factor estate tax exposure into your decision. This isn't just about your tax bill while you're alive — it's about what your family would face if something happened to you. Discuss this with both your US and India tax consultants before deciding what to keep in the US.

Other financial loose ends

  • HSA (Health Savings Account) — If you have one, you can keep it and use it for qualified medical expenses. The funds don't expire. You just can't contribute after losing your US health insurance.
  • Stock options / RSUs — Check your vesting schedule. Unvested RSUs are typically forfeited when you leave. Vested but unexercised stock options may have a limited exercise window (often 90 days) after your last day.
  • Student loans — These don't go away when you move. Set up autopay from your US account and budget for them in your India finances.
  • Subscriptions and recurring charges — Go through your credit card statements and cancel anything you won't use. Gym memberships, streaming services with US-only content, insurance policies — they add up.
