Finalize the Why?

Last updated: February 2026

Moving back to India is one of the biggest decisions you'll make. Before diving into logistics, take the time to get brutally honest about why you're doing this. A clear "why" keeps you grounded when the process gets overwhelming — and it will.

Common reasons people move back

  • Family — Aging parents, wanting kids to grow up near grandparents, or simply missing the closeness of family life.
  • Career opportunity — India's tech and startup ecosystem has matured. Some roles pay competitively, especially in leadership or niche domains.
  • Lifestyle — The cost of living, household help, food, and social life can be dramatically better in India for certain lifestyles.
  • Belonging — After years abroad, many feel a pull to be "home" — culturally, socially, and emotionally.
  • Visa fatigue — H1B uncertainty, green card backlogs, and immigration stress can accelerate the decision.

Tip

Write your reasons down. Literally. When you're knee-deep in paperwork or having a rough week post-move, you'll want to revisit this list.

Questions to ask yourself

There's no right or wrong answer — but being honest now saves regret later.

  1. Is this my decision or external pressure? — Family expectations are real, but resentment builds if the move doesn't feel like your own choice.
  2. Am I running toward something or away from something? — Moving back to escape burnout or a bad situation doesn't fix those problems. India has its own version of them.
  3. Can I handle a significant lifestyle adjustment? — Infrastructure, pollution, bureaucracy, and pace of life are genuinely different. Nostalgia often glosses over the daily friction.
  4. What does my partner/family think? — If you're not aligned with your spouse or immediate family, the move will strain relationships.
  5. What's my financial runway? — Even if you have a job lined up, the transition period costs more than you expect.

The "trial run" approach

If you're on the fence, consider spending 2-4 weeks in India — not as a vacation, but as a simulation. Work remotely if you can, deal with daily errands, visit hospitals, open a bank account. It's the fastest way to pressure-test your decision.

Use the trip to tackle some long-overdue government document updates — update your address on your voter ID, correct your name on an old document, apply for something at a government office. Try doing it both online and in person. See how the process feels — the wait times, the paperwork, the number of trips required. If you're comfortable with how things progress, that's a good sign. If it leaves you deeply frustrated, that's useful information too.

This is also a good time to test a low-cost US SIM card (Google Fi, Ultra Mobile, Tello, etc.) in India. Pop it in your phone and verify that SMS OTPs and 2FA codes come through reliably in the area you plan to live. Check Wi-Fi calling quality too. Knowing this works before you commit to a carrier and a move saves you from a painful surprise later.

Try using the US credit cards you plan to keep for everyday spending — groceries, restaurants, fuel, online orders. See how widely they're accepted in your area, whether the foreign transaction fees make them impractical, and if the rewards you earn are actually redeemable for things useful in India. This is the best way to figure out which cards are worth holding on to and which ones you can close before the move.

Warning

A two-week vacation to India is not the same as living there. Holidays filter out the frustrations. Make sure your trial run includes mundane, everyday life.

When you're not 100% sure

Almost nobody is 100% sure. That's normal. What matters is that your core reasons are strong enough to carry you through the hard parts. If your "why" is clear and deeply personal, the logistics become solvable problems rather than deal-breakers.

Note

Moving back isn't always easily reversible. Especially for H1-B holders, returning to the US means finding a new sponsor — and that's significantly harder to do from outside the country. Factor this into your decision and don't assume you can simply "go back if it doesn't work out."
