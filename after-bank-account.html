Open Indian Bank Account

Last updated: February 2026

A resident Indian bank account is essential for receiving salary, paying bills, making UPI payments, and managing daily finances. If you already had NRE/NRO accounts, those will need to be converted. If you're starting fresh, you'll need to open a new account.

If you have existing NRE/NRO accounts

Once your residential status changes to resident Indian (for tax purposes), you are required to inform your bank and convert your accounts:

  • NRE account — Must be converted to a regular resident savings account. The tax-free status on interest ends upon conversion. Any fixed deposits in NRE will continue at the original rate until maturity, after which they convert to regular FDs.
  • NRO account — Can be converted to a regular resident savings account. The process is usually simpler since NRO already had domestic tax treatment.
  • FCNR deposits — Foreign currency deposits will continue until maturity. On maturity, the proceeds are credited to your resident account in INR at the prevailing exchange rate.

Warning

Don't delay this conversion. Banks are required to redesignate accounts when your residency status changes. Continuing to operate NRE accounts as a resident can attract penalties and tax complications — the tax-free interest benefit is only for non-residents.

Opening a new account

If you don't have an existing Indian bank account, here's what to expect:

Documents required

  • PAN card (mandatory for all bank accounts in India)
  • Aadhaar card (for KYC and address verification)
  • Passport-size photographs
  • Proof of address — Aadhaar is usually sufficient. Some banks may ask for a utility bill or rental agreement.

Choosing a bank

  • Large private banks (HDFC, ICICI, Kotak, Axis) — Better digital experience, more responsive customer service, good mobile apps. Higher minimum balance requirements (₹10,000–₹25,000 depending on account type and city).
  • Public sector banks (SBI, Bank of Baroda, PNB) — Lower minimum balance requirements, wider branch network in smaller towns. Digital experience is improving but generally behind private banks.
  • Digital-first banks (Fi, Jupiter, Niyo) — Good app experience and usually zero minimum balance. These operate on top of partner banks (e.g., Fi uses Federal Bank). Useful as a secondary account.

Tip

Consider opening a salary account if your employer has a tie-up with a specific bank. Salary accounts typically have zero minimum balance requirements and come with perks like free debit cards and higher transaction limits.

Setting up UPI

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) is how India pays for nearly everything — from street food to rent. Once your bank account is active:

  1. Download a UPI app — Google Pay, PhonePe, Paytm, or your bank's own app.
  2. Register with your Indian mobile number (must be the one linked to your bank account).
  3. Add your bank account and set a UPI PIN.
  4. You're ready to send and receive payments instantly.

Note

UPI requires your mobile number to be linked to your bank account. If you opened the account with a different number or are still using a US number, visit the branch and update your registered mobile number first. UPI will not work without this.

Other things to set up

  • Internet banking and mobile banking — Register at the branch or through the bank's app. You'll need this for fund transfers, bill payments, and managing fixed deposits.
  • Debit card — Most banks issue one with the account. Activate it and set your ATM PIN. Consider a RuPay card for domestic use (lower merchant fees) or a Visa/Mastercard debit if you need international usability.
  • Auto-pay mandates — Set up standing instructions for recurring payments like rent, utilities, insurance premiums, and SIPs (systematic investment plans).
